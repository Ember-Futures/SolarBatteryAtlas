Harnessing the Sun

How solar and batteries are turning intermittent sunshine into firm power

By Daan Walter, Kingsmill Bond, Sam Butler-Sloss, and Antoine Issac • June 19th 2026
expand_more Scroll to explore

Introduction

For most of solar’s history, the main argument against it was cost. Solar panels were too expensive, and adding batteries to make supply dependable was more expensive still.

That has changed. Solar and batteries have followed the familiar path of manufactured technologies: deployment rose, costs fell, and new markets opened. In much of the world, solar is now among the cheapest sources of new electricity.

The harder challenge now is reliability. Solar produces when the sun is available, while demand continues through evenings, nights and weak-sun periods. Batteries can help close that gap, but the scale of what they can do remains widely underestimated.

We built a new model to test this reliability question directly. We divided the world into roughly 5,000 regions, each about the size of Belgium, and asked what it would take for solar and batteries to serve a constant 1 MW load in every hour of the year. The model includes no demand shifting, no grid imports, no wind, no hydro and no backup. Just solar, batteries and a flat load.

This is a deliberately strict test. Real power systems have more options: demand varies by hour, grids trade across regions, customers can shift some use, and complementary technologies already exist. A flat load gives a hard and conservative benchmark for how close solar and batteries can get to firm power on their own.

The results show that solar plus batteries can already provide high-uptime electricity across much of the world, at costs that are competitive in many of the places where demand is growing fastest. This article sets out nine findings.

1. Sunlight is abundant everywhere

We start with sizing the total potential of solar.. What is the potential of solar around the world?

The answer is simply: enormous. The sun supplies the Earth with thousands of times more energy than humanity uses. Even after applying limits on usable land, build density and conversion efficiency, solar potential remains far larger than local electricity demand in most regions.

The map to the right measures each region's constrained annual solar potential as a multiple of its local electricity demand. More than nine in ten people live in regions where that potential is at least ten times local demand; for over half of humanity it is more than a hundred times. In absolute terms, the scale is striking: more than 95% of the global population lives in regions where local solar potential per person is over twice current U.S. electricity demand per person, about 12 MWh/year. For roughly two-thirds of humanity, local solar potential per person is even greater than Saudi Arabia’s oil production per person.

Globally, technically suitable land alone could generate around 185 times today's electricity consumption, and still about 125 times after stricter land-use exclusions (the "Policy" toggle).

This abundance is central to the rest of the analysis. In effectively every region on Earth, the question is not whether enough solar energy can be harvested over the course of a year; the annual potential is vast. The real constraint is timing: making that energy available in the hours when it is needed. Solve that timing problem, and the usable solar resource becomes enormous.

2. Batteries make all the difference

Once solar is cheap, overbuilding becomes the natural first lever to raise uptime. That works for a long time. More capacity raises annual generation, improves output in weaker hours and seasons, and increases the share of demand solar can serve.

But only overbuildign solar eventually runs into limits. Additional panels keep adding energy, yet less of it arrives in the hours that set uptime. In our simulations, even a twenty-fold overbuild never carries a solar-only system past about 47% of a flat load, anywhere on Earth. Annual generation becomes abundant while reliability stays capped by timing.

Batteries are the game changer. They turn curtailed output into usable supply and move solar from a resource that can cover a large share of energy demand into one that can cover a large share of hours. Watch the map as the battery grows from 0 to 24 MWh: uptime fills in, evenings first, then nights, then the weak-sun days within a week. Regions home to more than 90% of the world's population can reach 80% uptime, large parts of the world move into the 80–90% range, and favourable regions push past 95%, without any other backup.

This is why the relevant unit of analysis is not solar alone, but a solar-battery system: cheap generation paired with enough storage to shift it into the hours when it is needed.

3. Batteries work because most solar variability is daily

Batteries have such a large effect because, for most of the world, the dominant solar balancing problem is daily rather than seasonal.

Seasonality matters, especially at higher latitudes. It sets much of the difficulty in reaching very high uptime in places with weak winter output. Across most populated regions, however, the recurring day-night cycle accounts for the largest and most predictable gap between solar output and a flat load.

This makes short-duration storage unusually powerful. A few hours of storage can absorb a large share of otherwise curtailed output and shift it into the hours that determine uptime. The system does not need to solve seasonal storage before solar-plus-battery becomes valuable; it first needs to solve the daily cycle.

The hourly profiles show this most clearly. In the weekly animation, yellow represents solar used as it is produced and purple is the same sunlight served back after dark. Switch seasons and the seasonal swing is visible, but the main swing pattern remains the daily surplus and deficit of day/night. Batteries are well matched to that pattern, which is why they lift uptime so sharply across much of the world.

4. Solar plus batteries are cheap exactly where most people live

Having covered the physics, let us turn to economics. It is increasingly accepted that solar-plus-battery systems are now cheap. What is less widely understood is that they are cheapest precisely where most people on Earth live.

When we run the numbers using today’s solar and battery costs from the IEA and BNEF, the result is striking: around 80% of the global population can get 80% uptime solar-plus-storage power for less than $100/MWh, and for half of humanity the cost is below about $80/MWh. That is already competitive with the average cost of power generation today, which is typically around $100/MWh. This is also a strict benchmark: it assumes a flat demand profile, with no demand shifting, no grid imports, and no other flexibility options.

The reason for the economic potential of solar is simple geography. Cheap solar-plus-storage requires enough sunlight, manageable seasonality and low enough system costs. That combination is common across the global sunbelt: India, Southeast Asia, much of Africa, Latin America, the Middle East, and parts of southern Europe and North America.

More than 90% of the global population lives between 45 degrees north and 45 degrees south. A large share of current electricity demand sits in regions where the solar resource is good enough and the seasonal profile is manageable. As we will see, future demand growth is even more concentrated there.

Of course good geography does not always come with cheap capital. Many of the regions with the strongest solar resources also face higher borrowing costs, raising the cost of capital and the delivered cost of electricity. The map’s local-cost view shows how much this impacts cost. Even then, the central result holds: across much of the emerging world, solar-plus-storage remains structurally low-cost because the underlying resource is so strong.

5. Solar plus batteries are cheap where electricity is missing

Around 760 million people still lack access to electricity. Their current options are no electricity, kerosene, diesel generators, battery charging shops, or waiting years for grid extension.

Many of these communities, shown in red on the map, are in regions with strong sunlight, mild seasonality, and low solar-plus-storage costs. In other words, many people still without electricity live in exactly the places where solar and batteries can already provide some of the cheapest power on Earth. Some 89% of people without access live where that 80%-uptime cost is under $80/MWh; effectively 100% under $100/MWh.

The advantage is not only cost, but proximity. Local solar and batteries can generate, store, and deliver electricity close to demand, reducing the need to extend centralised infrastructure across long distances before service can begin.

The sun has reaches every village in the world every day. The technologies to harvest and store its energy have now become cheap enough to matter, and can provide affordable electricity for all.

6. Solar plus batteries can already beat many grids on uptime

An even larger group in emerging economies do have an electricity connection, yet still have very poor service. In our data, close to 2 billion people are connected to grids that deliver less than 99% uptime; counting those with no access at all, over 2.5 billion people live without dependable electricity. They face outages, load shedding, voltage swings, damaged appliances, lost business hours and high spending on emergency backup.

For these customers, the benchmark for solar plus battery to beat is the grid they actually experience. In many places, 80–90% uptime from solar plus batteries would be a major improvement, especially if it comes with lower delivered costs and reduced diesel use. The left chart counts the people at each level of grid reliability; the right chart shows what it costs to match that reliability with solar and storage instead. It shows how nine out of ten people could be served more reliably by solar and storage at a price at or below$70/MWh.

Solar plus batteries do not need to match the world's best grids before they become useful. They can rapidly scale in markets where the incumbent system currently fails to deliver reliable power.

This creates a large early market from which to scale. This is not just theoretical: solar and battery uptake is surging across emerging markets today, as seen in countries like India, Pakistan, and Kenya.

7. Getting from 90–95% to 100% needn't be expensive

Solar plus batteries can get close to firm power in many regions. The cost challenge rises sharply only when solar and batteries are required to cover every last hour by themselves. Pushing from 95% to 99% uptime raises the typical cost by around $10-15/MWh, a roughly 20% increase, and in harder regions by $30/MWh or more. Pushing from 99% to 100% can be more than double this amount.

The constraint is utilisation. The final few percent of hours are rare. Covering them with only solar and batteries requires extra panels and storage that sit unused for most of the year.

A real power system has more options. Once solar plus batteries provide 90–95% of annual supply, the remaining hours can be covered by a cheap firming resource such as a gas turbine, diesel generator, hydro, interconnection, demand response or another dispatchable technology.

The simplest case is a gas turbine or diesel generator. They are cheap and only need to run when solar and batteries fall short, perhaps 5–10% of the year. Because these plants are cheap to build and run so rarely, their costs are modest when spread across the whole electricity supply.

In most regions, adding gas or diesel backup to solar plus battery to turn 90–95% uptime to 100% uptime only raises cost by about $10–25/MWh. Only $3–8/MWh of that is needed to pay for the backup plant itself; the rest is fuel. The reason the cost stays modest is that solar and batteries still provide the bulk of the energy, while the backup unit only covers the small tail of hours when they fall short.

8. Planned fossil capacity is exposed

Many countries are still planning new coal and gas capacity for baseload generation to meet rising electricity demand. A large share of this planned capacity is located in regions where solar plus batteries are already cheap.

Of the roughly 850 GW of planned coal and gas capacity in the GEM dataset, around 590 GW, about 70%, sits in regions where solar plus batteries can already deliver 80% uptime power for less than $100/MWh. The chart stacks that planned capacity against the cost of the solar alternative in the same place. These are often regions with fast demand growth, strong solar resources, growing populations and weak or incomplete grids.

This does not imply a simple one-for-one replacement of every planned plant. Power systems need capacity, flexibility, reserves and grid infrastructure. But the default assumption that rising electricity demand requires new fossil capacity is increasingly outdated in many regions. Instead of a baseload coal plant, perhaps a solar plus battery site at 75% uptime also suffices. Or instead of an expensive baseload gas plant, a cheaper back-up plant plus solar and battery for the bulk of power may be a better fit.

Planners now need to compare new coal and gas plants against solar-plus-storage systems that can provide high-uptime electricity at competitive cost. In many growth markets, that comparison will show that solar plus battery beats any pure-play fossil projects on cost now.

9. Falling costs turn affordable into irresistible

All of the analysis so far uses today's costs. Solar and batteries are manufactured technologies, and their costs are still falling.

Deployment increases manufacturing scale. Scale reduces costs. Lower costs open new markets. Those markets support further deployment. Solar has followed this path for decades, and batteries are now moving through a similar phase.

Today's affordability map is therefore not fixed. As time advances toward 2050, regions that are already cheap become cheaper, and regions just outside competitiveness cross the line. The global area where solar plus batteries can provide high-uptime electricity at low cost expands over time.

By 2030, our analysis suggests that more than three quarters of the world's population could get 80% uptime solar-plus-storage power for less than $80/MWh, and 90% of the world for less than $100/MWh.

Power plants are long-lived assets. A coal or gas plant planned today will operate for decades. During that time, the solar-plus-storage alternative is likely to keep getting cheaper and cheaper. What is competitive today will become irresistible in a decade.

Conclusion

The old debate was about whether solar was cheap enough. That question has largely been answered. The remaining debate is reliability: how far solar and batteries can go in providing dependable power, and at what cost.

The results suggest they can go much further than most power planning assumes. Solar resources are large enough almost everywhere. Overbuilding turns cheap sunlight into abundant annual energy. Batteries move that energy into the hours when it is needed. Together, they can already supply high-uptime power across much of the world, especially where people live, where grids are weakest, and where demand is growing fastest.

Where solar plus batteries reach 90–95% uptime, the last few percent of hours can often be covered with a small amount of dispatchable backup at modest incremental cost. That turns 100% uptime from a fatal objection into a system design problem.

The implications are largest in emerging economies. Solar plus batteries are cheap where electricity is still missing, can outperform many unreliable grids, and are already competitive in many regions where new fossil capacity is planned.

And this is before the next decade of cost declines. Every year, solar and batteries get cheaper; every year, the case for building around them gets stronger. The question is no longer whether the sun can power the world. It is how quickly we build the systems that let it.
