Harnessing the Sun

How solar and batteries are turning intermittent sunshine into firm power

By Daan Walter, Kingsmill Bond, Sam Butler-Sloss, and Antoine Issac • June 19th 2026
expand_more Scroll to explore

Introduction

In most of the world, new solar is now among the cheapest ways to make electricity. While few can argue with that point anymore, a second one has taken its place: solar is cheap, but it cannot be trusted to keep the lights on by itself.

In this article we investigate this claim. How much always-on electricity could can be supplied by solar plus battery, and how does that differ across the world?

To answer it, we cut the world into roughly 5,000 regions, each about the size of Belgium or Maryland. In every one we ask the hardest possible version of the question: can solar and storage alone serve a flat 1 MW load, the same power in every hour of the year, with nowhere to hide?

A flat load is deliberately unfair. Real demand bends toward daylight, and real grids lean on trade, diversity, and flexible customers. We strip all of that away on purpose, because it removes the easy escape of moving demand to sunny hours. If solar and batteries can hold a constant load for most of the year under that constraint, a real system, with all its extra options, can only do better.

What follows are ten key results from our analysis. Together they show how much of the world can already run on firm, always-on power from solar and batteries alone, and what it costs.

1. Solar energy is abundant everywhere

We start with the simplest question, the one that ignores timing entirely: is there enough sunlight on usable land to match what people actually consume locally?

For most of the world the answer is yes, and not by a little. The map the the right measures each region's annual solar potential as a multiple of its local electricity demand. Apply realistic limits for suitable land, sensible build-out density, and real conversion efficiencies, and most regions still come out far ahead, many by 10×, 100×, even 1000×.

This abundance is not confined to the obvious places. The sunniest deserts and the tropics have the most, but temperate and higher-latitude regions also hold many times their own demand, given feasible land. They simply need a bit more of it to get there.

So the volume is not the problem. There is far more sunlight than we need almost everywhere. The problem is timing: getting the energy to the hour it is wanted. That is what the rest of this article is about.

2. Batteries make the sun shine at night

Solar generation varies in two main ways. There is the daily swing of day and night, and the slower seasonal swing of summer and winter. The daily swing is by far the larger of the two, and it is the one batteries are built to solve.

A battery takes the midday surplus, the solar that would otherwise be thrown away, and moves it to the hours after dark. In the weekly profile, the yellow is power used as the sun produces it, and the purple beneath it is that same sunlight, stored and served back at night. The season you select shifts the picture, and the seasonal swing is real, but the day-to-night gap dominates, and storage closes most of it.

As storage grows, two things happen at once, both mechanically. Less solar is wasted, because more of the midday peak is captured. And the delivered power gets steadier hour to hour, until a jagged solar signal starts to look like a flat line.

3. Batteries make all the difference

Solar is "intermittent," the story goes, so it must be propped up one-for-one by something firm before it counts as real power. The simulations show something different. Reliability comes in degrees, and storage is the dial.

As the battery grows from zero to 24 MWh, uptime fills in, in order: evenings first, then nights, then the weak-sun days that appear within any given week. A modest battery already carries many regions to 80 to 90% uptime.

The jump from roughly 60% to 90% uptime is not a small one. At 60%, power fails often enough that you still need a full backup for anything that matters. At 90%, it becomes dependable enough to run a home or a business on directly, with only rare gaps left to cover. Storage is what moves a region from the first state to the second.

4. High uptime solar plus batteries can be found in many places

High-uptime solar has a reputation as a boutique product: a handful of flagship projects in exceptionally sunny places, and not much else.

So we tested it. We took one fixed system, the configuration behind a landmark project completed last year in the desert of the United Arab Emirates (6 MW DC of solar paired with ~20 MWh of storage), and applied that identical setup to all ~5,000 regions, with no local tuning. The map shows what each one delivers.

The result is not a scatter of dots. Very high uptime spreads across the tropics and subtropics and reaches well into temperate latitudes. What makes it work is the pairing of decent average sunlight with a seasonal swing small enough for the battery to absorb. Strong desert sun helps, but it is not required, and that pairing turns out to be common across much of the world.

5. High uptime solar is cheap exactly where most people live

The best sunlight on Earth sits in empty deserts. But economic viability does not need the best resource, only one that is good enough, paired with costs that are low enough. That combination is far more widespread than the best-resource map suggests.

On global-average costs for panels, batteries, and financing, the cheapest high-uptime power clusters along the global sunbelt: India, Southeast Asia, much of sub-Saharan Africa, and Latin America. These low-cost, high-uptime regions are home to billions of people. The cheapest firm solar shines on many of the world's most populous places.

Using one global cost set first is deliberate. It isolates the physical signal, showing where sunlight and seasonality alone make solar cheapest. Layering in local costs then adds the macroeconomic reality on top, above all the cost of capital, which varies enormously between countries and can make or break a capital-heavy project. The dollar figures shift, sometimes sharply, but the underlying geography holds: where the sun is strong and the seasons are mild, solar-plus-storage stays among the cheapest firm power anywhere. That gap between global and local also points to an opportunity. Where local financing is expensive, bringing the cost of capital down would unlock even cheaper solar-plus-storage on top of the physical resource already there.

6. High uptime solar can help resolve the last mile problem of electricity access

In much of the low- and middle-income world, the constraint on electricity is rarely just generation. Generation is often short too, but the harder problem is delivery: grid reach, grid quality, and the cost of extending and maintaining distribution out to dispersed customers. Even where power is produced, reliable supply can lag for years.

These same regions, shaded red here for the highest concentrations of people without dependable access, are the ones that performed well earlier for solar-plus-storage: strong yields, mild seasons. The resource is best where the grid is weakest.

For those communities, the honest comparison is not solar against a flawless grid. It is solar-plus-storage against the service people actually receive today, which is often little or none. Measured that way, local solar does not have to be perfect. It has to beat nothing, and it has to be affordable. It can now do both.

7. Even without backup, solar plus battery reliability can already exceed many grids

The last section looked at people with no electricity at all. There is a second, larger group: the billions who have a connection on paper but live with a grid that fails constantly. Outages, load shedding, and voltage swings are common enough that households and businesses already pay for backup: diesel generators, home battery units, or the plain cost of going dark. The left chart counts them. More than 2 billion people live below the >99% uptime that wealthy countries take for granted, and many endure grid uptime well below 70 or 80%.

Making a centralized grid that reliable is possible, but expensive, because reliability has to be built into every layer: spare generation, redundant transmission, hardened distribution, and last-mile maintenance that never stops.

The right chart shows what it costs to match each level of grid reliability with solar and storage instead. For most of these customers, near-perfect uptime was never the benchmark, because they have never had it. The benchmark is the power they get today, all-in: tariffs, fees, damaged appliances, downtime, and the spending it takes to bridge an outage. Against that, solar-plus-battery does not need to beat the world's best grids. It needs to be more dependable than the grid in front of the customer, and cheaper per usable kilowatt-hour than what they buy now.

This is the textbook opening from Christensen's The Innovator's Dilemma: a vast, under-served market the incumbents do not want. Utilities and grid planners are built to optimise dense demand, regulated returns, and large projects, not millions of dispersed, price-sensitive customers who need power that is good enough, cheap enough, now.

In that market, solar-plus-battery is already good enough, because it delivers higher uptime and lower delivered cost at the same time. For the first time these customers can buy power that is both more reliable and cheaper than what they have. That is what lets it take hold, and from there the familiar pattern follows: costs keep falling, performance keeps climbing, and a technology that began as backup grows into primary power.

8. Reaching 100% uptime needn't be expensive

The last map stopped just short of a perfect 100%, and for good reason. Squeezing the final few percent out of solar and batteries alone is brutally expensive: it takes a pile of extra panels and storage that sit idle almost the entire year, earning their keep only on the dimmest stretches.

But solar and batteries do not have to close that gap by themselves. A cheap generator, a diesel genset at a few hundred dollars per kilowatt, can cover it. The genset itself adds only about 3 to 5 dollars per MWh to the blended cost of power. It runs just the 5 to 10% of the year when solar-plus-battery falls short, and the diesel it burns over those weeks adds roughly another 15 to 20. Even all-in, that backup raises the blended cost of power by only about 20 dollars per MWh. It works as cheap insurance for the worst stretches.

The map shows the all-in cost of a true 100%-uptime system once that backup fills the gap. Toggle it off and you see what solar-plus-battery reaches on its own, around 90%. Toggle it on and you reach 100%. The difference between the two, shown as back-up cost only, is the price of that last stretch, and across most of the sunbelt it stays small.

That redraws the reliability debate. "Firm" no longer has to mean a thermal plant grinding away around the clock. It can mean cheap, abundant solar, a battery for the daily cycle, and a small generator that wakes only for the small slice of the year when the sun falls short.

9. High uptime solar is cheap exactly where other "firm" capacity is being planned right now

Electricity demand is still climbing, hardest in emerging economies. Their planning documents answer it the familiar way: gigawatts of new "firm" capacity, mostly coal, gas, and nuclear, booked years in advance.

Those plants are concentrated in precisely the climates where solar-plus-storage already reaches high uptime at low cost. The chart stacks that planned capacity by fuel against the cost of the solar alternative in the same place: bar by bar, gigawatts of coal, gas, and nuclear queued up in regions where firm solar is already cheaper. This overlap has a simple cause. The economies adding demand fastest, across South Asia, Southeast Asia, Africa, and Latin America, mostly sit at low latitudes where the sun is strong, so the fastest-growing demand and the best solar resource land in the same places.

This does not mean solar erases every plant on every grid. It means the reflex that firm power must burn something is no longer a safe default across a large and growing share of the world.

10. Costs will continue to fall and further increase competitiveness

Every map so far has used today's costs. That is the conservative case.

Solar and batteries have only ever gotten cheaper, and almost every outlook expects the fall to continue. The latest IEA and BNEF projections see the installed cost of solar and battery projects falling by as much as half by 2050. As the slider advances from 2024 toward 2050, regions that are already cheap get cheaper, and regions sitting just outside competitiveness cross the line into it.

The viability map is not static. It widens every year as costs fall, and the direction of travel only runs one way. Whatever the picture looks like today, the cost curve is still bending in solar's favour.

The path forward

Put together, the ten results point in a single direction. Solar energy is abundant almost everywhere, many times over what each region consumes. The problem was never the resource, only the timing, and batteries solve most of it. Paired with storage, solar can serve the large majority of a constant load, as much as 95% in many regions, deserts and temperate latitudes alike, with no backup at all.

That power is cheapest where most people live, and where grids today are weakest or missing entirely. It is already more dependable than the grid many of those people have. A small diesel generator closes the final gap to 100% cheaply: a few dollars per MWh for the genset, plus roughly 15 to 20 for the fuel it burns covering the last few percent. And it is cheap in exactly the places now planning new coal, gas, and nuclear, with costs still falling year after year.

So the physics works, and increasingly so do the economics. What remains is a question of speed: who clears the permitting queues first, fixes the financing, and rewrites rules still built for thermal plants. Firm power no longer has to burn anything. The question now is who races toward solar the fastest.
